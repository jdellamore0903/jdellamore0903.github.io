Debate

Debate will probably be my biggest love for the rest of my life. I began debating for my high school in Chicago during my junior year and then continued for four years in college. Currently, I am the Assistant Director of Debate at NYU, where I coach and travel across the country with the team.

While an entire page should be dedicated to why I love debate so much, I can generalize and say that I love debate because it offers infinite possibility to learn on a variety of subject matters across all fields. When most people think of debate, they see debaters arguing "eloquently" in suits, citing Benjamin Franklin or Thomas Moore. Over my 6 years competing, I never wore a suit, debated at a speed akin to an auctioneer and cited everyone from national security experts at the Brookings Institute to artists in the DADA art movement. Debate never allows you to know everything and will allow you to challenge conventional fact with a strong set of arguments. I am so happy to still be part of the community and hope I can be for years to come.

  • Postmodern Debate Arguments

    I have always been very interested in philosophical debate arguments called kritiques (spelled with a "k" to pay homage to Kant). Lately, I have gone further down the postmodernism path, reading and creating arguments based on works by Foucault, Lyotard and Baudrillard. I have always liked this body of work but this years topic on healthcare lends itself to many of these philosophical ideas such as biopolitics (Foucault) and simulations (Baudrillard). One particular interesting book I read recently was The Birth of the Clinic by Foucault. I really enjoyed the historical overview of how hospitals and the study of medicine evolved over time (with a focus on France).

  • Switch Side Web App

    Switch Side was my capstone project at Actualize Coding School. The jist fo the app is to create a structured debate forum online, akin to "policy debate" (the best type of debate). The structured arguments input by the users could not only provide an interesting format to guide future research but could also be an interesting way to generate data for natural language processing algorithms. What I mean by this is a "card" in debate is an argument provided by the user that summarizes the "card text", which is an academic or journalistic article. Interestingly enough, in debate we call the first part of the "card" a tag, which is exactly what it would be called within a training data set. The data set of article text tagged with a summary may provide an interest data set for programs that try to automate a portion of the research process.
